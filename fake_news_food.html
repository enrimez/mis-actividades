Real or Fake News?

Can you tell which food news stories are true and which are fake?

🔍 How to spot fake news

Before reading the news stories, learn to recognise the warning signs. Fake news uses clever tricks to fool us!

🚨

Alarming or sensational headlines

Fake news uses scary, exaggerated titles designed to grab your attention at all costs. If a headline seems impossible or makes you feel very surprised, be suspicious.

🖼️

Suspicious images

The photos are often montages or blurry, unprofessional images. Sometimes they are illustrations or cartoons instead of real photographs. You can check by searching the image on Google Images.

📋

No sources or dates

Fake news doesn't say who wrote it, when it happened, or where the information comes from. Real news always includes the journalist's name, the date, and the original source.

📤

Asking you to share it

If a story says "forward this to all your contacts" or "share before they delete this!", that is a very clear warning sign. Real news never does that.

🔗

Fake-looking links and names

Sometimes they use web addresses very similar to trusted news sites, like bbc-health-today24.com instead of bbc.co.uk. Always read the address carefully before trusting it!

💡

What to do if you're suspicious: Search the claim on NHS.uk, WHO.int or FullFact.org. Look for the same story in other reliable newspapers. Ask a trusted adult — a teacher, parent or doctor. Remember: if it sounds too good or too scary to be true, check it first!
